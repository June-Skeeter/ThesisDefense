Introduction: Framing the Problem

Arctic amplification is accelerating climate change in the northern high latitudes, leading to:

  • Decreased snow and ice cover
  • Changing precipitation patterns
  • Permafrost degradation
  • Extending growing seasons
  • Changing carbon (C) balances
    • Influences the rate of warming globally
Data Source: IPCC (2021)

Limited Observations

Eddy covariance (EC) is a method to measure carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4) fluxes.

  • Sparse coverage in the Arctic
    • Bias towards accessible sites
    • Canadian Arctic is under-represented
Data Sources: Walker et al. (2005); Pallandt et al. (2021)

Tundra Ecosystems

Harsh conditions limit vegetation growth.

  • Summers are brief but productive
  • Winters are long, dark, and frigid
Reindeer grazing on willow shrubs at Illisarvik

Tundra Ecosystems

Permafrost is widespread in tundra environments.

  • Root growth and microbial activity occur in the active layer
    • Thaws seasonally
  • Restricted drainage leads to elevated water tables
    • Inhibits decomposition

Tundra Carbon Balance

Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE) of CO2 is the primary component.

  • NEE = ER - GPP
    • GPP: Gross Primary Productivity
      • Photosynthetic uptake of CO2
    • ER: Ecosystem Respiration
      • Autotrophic (RA) + Heterotrophic (RH)
  • -/+ NEE (uptake/emission)

Tundra Carbon Balance

Net Methane Exchange (NEE) of CH4 is a secondary component.

  • NME = Production - Consumption
    • Methanogenesis in anoxic soils
      • Slower pathway
    • Methanotropy in aerobic soils
    • Complex non-linear dynamics
  • -/+ NME (uptake/emission)

Mackenzie Delta

The Arctic's 2nd largest river delta.

  • Aircraft observations of NME (Kohnert et al. 2017 & 2018)
    • Gas hydrate seeps in south / west
    • Significant biologic emissions north of tree line (31.8 - 58.4 mg CH4 m−2 d−1)
  • No prior studies of NEE

Study Sites

Fluxes of CO2 and CH4 were measured at two sites.

  • Illisarvik in 2016
    • Drained Thermokarst Lake Basin (DTLB)
  • Fish Island in 2017
    • Low Center Polygon (LCP) tundra peatland

Gap Filling

Gap filling is essential for calculating daily/seasonal/annual budgets from EC observations.

  • Flux partitioning is the standard approach for NEE
    • Night-time methods not possible during polar summer
    • Rapid phenology changes make day-time methods not impractical too
  • No standard methods for NME
  • Machine Learning approaches
    • Neural Network (NN) or Random Forest (RF) models

Research Objectives

Advance our understanding of the dynamics governing growing season C exchange in the Mackenzie Delta Region.

  1. Measure CO2 and CH4 fluxes and use gap-filling to estimate NEE and NME at Illisarvik and Fish Island
  2. Use NN models to study controls of ecosystem scale NEE and NME
    1. Identify flux drivers and map functional relationships
    2. Investigate the influence of spatial heterogeneity
  3. Conduct a temporal upscaling experiment to study the possible influence of climate variability on growing season NEE and NME at Fish Island

Results

Observations over the peak growing season in 2016.

  • Mean Ta 9.5 °C
  • Gradual drying
    • limited precipitation
  • The site was a net CO2 sink
    • -1.56 CI95% ±0.17 μmol m-2s-1
  • The site was a weak CH4 source
    • 8.67 CI95% ±0.43 nmol m-2s-1

Flux response functions

Four key drivers of NEE:
PPFD, VPD, VWC, & FShrub.

  • NEE responds to PPFD as expected
    • Highlights influence of VPD
    • Not a true "light response curve"
      • GPP is not isolated
      • Conflated with ER
  • VWC influences ER
    • Night (dashed) vs. day (solid)
    • Contrary influence depending on source area

Results

Five key drivers of NNE:
FSedge, VWC, Ts, FShrub & U.

  • NME responds to VWC as expected
    • Highlights influence of source area
      • Projection to 100% FSedge
      • Supported by chamber results
  • Inverse association with TS
    • Possibly byproduct of short sampling period
    • Drying + increased methanotrophy

Discussion

Peak growing season C uptake at Illisarvik is greater than DTLB young studied in Alaska.

  • NME was lower than observed in other young DTLB
  • Illisarvik will continue to evolve, taking one of two paths:
    • Sedge wetlands
      • NME will increase significantly
    • Shrub dominated
      • NME would remain similar to current levels
  • Long-term observation needed to better understand the implications
    • Effects of changing shrub/sedge cover on NEE are less straightforward

Data & Methods

Neural Network models were used to:

  • Identify the dominant flux drivers
    • Pruned over-parametrized models using the Weights Method (Gevery et al. 2003)
      • 21 climate, soil, and source area inputs
      • Calculated Relative Importance (RI) from sum of the squared partial derivatives
        • Removed inputs with RI < 2.5%
  • Pruned models were re-trained
    • Used to estimate NEE and NME
    • The RI and partial derivatives were inspected
      • More explicit and detailed than Chapter 2

Methane only had a minor impact on net C uptake (-49.5 g C m-2).

Chapter 4: Modeling Interannual Variability of Carbon Fluxes at a Low-Center Polygon Ecosystem in the Mackenzie River Delta

A version of chapter 4 has been submitted to Arctic Science and is currently under review

Objectives

The observations from Fish Island help to fill a knowledge gap.

  • However, one growing season of data is insufficient
    • Observations require more context
  • Eleven years of climate data are available for Fish Island
    • The EC station was close to an automated weather station (AWS)
      • RN, Ta, U, and Rainfall
      • Can be used to provide context and expand upon observations

Data & Methods

A Flux Driver Time Series (FDTS) was constructed to conduct a temporal upscaling experiment.

  • Spanned 2009 - 2019 "snow-free" seasons
  • AWS data paired with ECMWF reanalysis and MODIS data
  • Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression models were trained on 2017 bio-climate data
    • Estimated NEE and NME using NN models described in Chapter 3

Temporal upscaling indicated significant interannual variability in growing season C exchange.

  • Highlights need for long-term monitoring

Chapter 5: Conclusions

Summary of findings, key contributions, future research, and reflections

Summary of Findings

Both sites were growing season CO2 sinks and CH4 sources.

  • Illisarvik:
    • Greater CO2 uptake and less CH4 emission than other DTLB studied
  • Fish Island:
    • Higher CH4 fluxes than LCP ecosystems in Alaska and Siberia
    • Mixed results for CO2 uptake

Summary of Findings

Spatial heterogeneity had significant influence over fluxes at both sites.

  • Illisarvik:
    • Both NEE and NME varied between different vegetation types
  • Fish Island:
    • Polygonal microtopography had a significant influence over NME
    • Less significant influence over NEE

Methodological Contributions

Neural Networks are not "black-box" models when applied carefully.

  • The weights method helps us understand what the models are actually doing
    • To date, had not been applied to EC data
  • Mapping response functions is a key advantage over Random Forest Models
  • Meticulous application is the key to success
    • Repetition (ensembles, bootstrapping, etc.)
    • Cross validation and early stopping
  • Can be paired with footprint modelling to help account for spatial heterogeneity

Future Research

  • Long-term flux data needed:
    • Chapter 4 shows that interannual variability can have a significant impact on fluxes
    • Warming may reduce overall sink strength
  • Cold season fluxes:
    • Extended senescent period could offset much of growing season uptake
  • Methane fluxes:
    • Pressure pumping
    • Spatial variability and landscape scale changes
  • Network architecture
    • Multi-layer models to simulate GPP and ER separately
    • Time-lagged models to account for CH4 dynamics

Reflections

Future work in the Arctic should focus on long-term flux monitoring.

  • Short, resource intensive campaigns are not sustainable
  • Prioritize involvement of local stakeholders
    • Technical training & employment
    • Engagement and education
    • Community support = longer-term viability

Machine Learning Methods

Random Forests (RF):

  • Ensembles of bootstrapped regression trees
    • Easy to train and implement
    • Resistant to over fitting
  • Limitations
    • Incapable of extrapolation
    • No interpretable response functions

Neural Networks (NN):

  • Computationally intensive non-linear regression
    • Universal approximators
    • Prone to over fitting
  • Weights method (Gevery et al. 2003)
    • Inspection of response functions
    • Verify physical plausibility

Chapter 2

Neural Network models were used to:

  • Identify the dominant flux drivers by iteratively building more complex models
    • Climate, soil, and source area were used as model inputs
    • "1 Standard Error" rule identified the most parsimonious models
  • Selected sets of inputs for CO2 and CH4 were then used to gap fill the respective flux time series to estimate NEE and NME

Chapter 2

  • NEE: good agreement with FCO2 observations (r2 = 0.91)
    • PPFD was the primary driver of GPP
      • Modulated by VPD
    • VWC influenced ER
    • FShrub had weak influence
  • NME: reasonable agreement with FCH4 observations (r2 = 0.62)
    • FSedge was had strongest influence
      • Along with FShrub (strong correlation)
    • VWC and Ts drove fluxes
    • U had a weak influence
      • Possibly influenced transport
