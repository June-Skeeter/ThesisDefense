Framing the Problem

Arctic amplification is accelerating climate change in the northern high latitudes, leading to:

  • Decreased snow and ice cover
  • Changing precipitation patterns
  • Permafrost degradation
  • Extending growing seasons
  • Changing carbon (C) balances
    • Influences the rate of warming globally
Data Source: IPCC (2021)

Framing the Problem

Eddy covariance (EC) is a method to measure carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4) fluxes.

  • Sparse coverage in the Arctic
    • Bias towards accessible sites
    • Canadian Arctic is under-represented
  • Ecosystems characterized by limited productivity
    • Latitudinal gradient
    • Modulated by elevation and maritime influences
Data Sources: Walker et al. (2005); Pallandt et al. (2021)

Tundra Ecosystems

Harsh conditions limit vegetation growth in tundra ecosystems.

  • Summers are brief but productive
    • 24hr daylight
    • Rapid green up after snowmelt
    • Photosynthesis decreases after senescence
  • Winters are long, dark, and frigid
    • Microbial activity continue through early winter as soils freeze

Tundra Carbon Ballance

Permafrost limits root growth, inhibits decomposition, and restricts drainage.

  • The active layer thaws seasonally
    • Root growth and microbial activity
  • Elevated water tables
    • Further inhibit decomposition
  • Many tundra ecosystems are/were net carbon sinks

Net Ecosystem Exchange

CO2 fluxes are the primary component of the tundra C balance

  • NEE = ER - GPP
    • GPP: Gross Primary Productivity
      • Photosynthetic uptake of CO2
    • ER: Ecosystem Respiration
      • Autotrophic (RA) + Heterotrophic (RH)
  • CO2 uptake/emission (-/+ NEE)

Net Methane Exchange

CH4 fluxes are a secondary component in tundra wetlands.

  • Methanogenesis occurs in anoxic soils
    • Slower, but CH4 is a potent greenhouse gas
    • Methanotrophs breakdown CH4
  • CH4 uptake/emission (-/+ NME)

The Study Area

The Mackenzie Delta (13,000 km2) is the 2nd largest Arctic Delta.

  • One of the fastest warming regions in North America
  • No existing studies of NEE in the Delta
  • Aircraft observations of NME (Kohnert et al. 2017 & 2018)
    • Gas hydrate seeps in south / west
    • Significant biologic emissions north of tree line (31.8 - 58.4 mg CH4 m−2 d−1)

The Study Area

We measured CO2 and CH4 fluxes at two tundra sites.

  • Illisarvik is an experimentally Drained Thermokarst Lake Basin (DTLB) on Richards Island
  • Undisturbed Low center polygon (LCP) tundra on the Big Lake Delta Plain
    • Permafrost peatland subject to infrequent episodic flooding

Machine Learning

Algorithms that build models from experience without making prior assumptions about the distribution of a dataset.

  • EC flux estimation requires specific conditions that can't always be met
    • Stationarity, turbulent flow, etc.
    • Gap filling is essential for calculating daily/seasonal/annual budgets
    • Flux partitioning is the standard approach for NEE
      • Night-time methods not possible during polar summer
      • Rapid phenology changes make day-time methods not impractical too
    • No standard methods for NME
  • Machine learning methods can identify flux drivers and map responses

Machine Learning

Random Forests (RF): ensembles of bootstrapped regression trees which can be easily deployed and quickly trained.

  • Limitations: incapable of extrapolation, no interpretable response functions

Neural Networks (NN): computationally intensive non-linear regression models which can approximate any continuous function.

  • Care must be taken to prevent over fitting
  • Weights method: inspection of response functions (Gevery et al. 2003)
    • Verify physical plausibility
  • Potentially useful for extrapolation

Research Objectives

Advance our understanding of the dynamics governing growing season C exchange in the Mackenzie Delta Region.

  1. Measure growing season CO2 and CH4 fluxes at Illisarvik and Fish Island, use gap-filling to estimate NEE and NME, and quantify the portion of CO2 uptake offset by CH4 emissions.
  2. Use NN models to gain insight into the controls of ecosystem scale NEE and NME at Illisarvik and Fish Island.
    1. Identify bio-climate drivers and map their functional relationships.
    2. Investigate the influence of spatial heterogeneity over fluxes.
  3. Investigate inter-annual climate variability at Fish Island and conduct a temporal upscaling experiment to estimate the possible influence of variability on growing season NEE and NME.

Data & Methods

NN models were used to:

  • Identify the dominant flux drivers by iteratively built more complex models
    • Climate, soil, and source area were used as model inputs
    • "1 Standard Error" rule identified the most parsimonious models
  • Selected sets of inputs for CO2 and CH4 were then used to gap fill the respective flux time series to estimate NEE and NME

Results

Observations over the peak growing season in 2016.

  • The site was a net CO2 sink
    • NEE: -1.5 CI95% ±0.2 g C-CO2 m-2d-1
    • NN estimates in good agreement with FCO2 observations (r2 = 0.91)
  • The site was a weak CH4 source
    • NME: 8.7 CI95% ±0.4 mg C-CH4 m-2d-1
    • NN estimates in reasonable agreement with FCH4 observations (r2 = 0.62)

Results

Peak growing season drivers:

  • NEE: four key divers
    • PPFD was the primary driver of GPP
      • Modulated by VPD
    • VWC influenced ER
    • FShrub had weak influence
  • NME: five key divers
    • FSedge was had strongest influence
      • Along with FShrub (strong correlation)
    • VWC and Ts drove fluxes
    • U had a weak influence
      • Possibly influenced transport

Results

Mapping fluxes over the domain of one driver, with the others fixed gives the response function under specific conditions.

  • NEE responds to PPFD as expected
    • Highlights influence of VPD
    • Not a true "light response curve"
      • GPP is not isolated
  • VWC: night (dashed) vs. day (solid)
    • More positive at high FShrub
    • Less straightforward at low FShrub

Results

Mapping fluxes over the domain of one driver, with the others fixed gives the response function under specific conditions.

  • NME responds to VWC as expected
    • Highlights influence of source area
      • 100% FSedge is a projection
      • Supported by chamber results
  • Inverse association with T S
    • Unexpected, possibly byproduct of short sampling period

Conclusions

Peak growing season carbon uptake at Illisarvik is greater than similarly aged landscape features in Alaska.

  • NME is below levels observed in other DTLB studied
  • Illisarvik will continue to evolve, its trajectory could alter it's carbon balance
    • If it follows the trajectory of older DTLB in the area and becomes a sedge wetlands
      • NME will increase significantly
    • If it transitions into a shrub dominated DTLB
      • NME would remain similar to current levels
    • Effects of changing shrub/sedge cover on NEE are less straightforward
  • Longer term observation needed to better understand the implications of vegetation change on DTLB carbon balances

Data & Methods

NN models were used to:

  • Identified the dominant flux drivers by pruning over-parametrized models
    • 21 climate, soil, and source area inputs were used
    • Weights method (Gevery et al. 2003) was used for pruning
      • Removed inputs with a Relative Importance (RI) < 2.5%
      • RI is based on the sum of the squared partial derivatives
  • New models were trained with the remaining inputs for CO2 and CH4 respectively
    • Used to gap fill the flux time series to estimate NEE and NME
    • The RI and partial derivatives were inspected
      • More explicit look at functional relationships than methods from Chapter 2

Conditions over the 2017 growing season.

Discussion & Conclusions

Methane had a minor impact on net C uptake (-49.5 g C m-2).

  • NME matches well with aircraft fluxes in the area (Kohnert et al. 2017)
  • Spatial heterogeneity had a significant impact on NME
  • NEE was more negative than similar landscapes
    • Growing season may be more productive than LCP ecosystems

Chapter 4: Controls on Carbon Dioxide and Methane fluxes from a Low-Center Polygonal Peatland in the Mackenzie River Delta

A version of chapter 4 has been submitted to Arctic Science and is currently under review

Data & Methods

Eleven yeas of climate data were available from an automated weather station (AWS) at Fish Island

  • Data was paired with ECMWF reanalysis and MODIS data to conduct a temporal upscaling experiment
    • Constructed a Flux Driver Time Series spanning "snow free" seasons from 2009 - 2019
      • Investigated inter-annual variability
      • Estimated NEE and NME using NN models described in Chapter 3
      • Compared growing seasons to shoulder seasons
  • Additional LANDSAT8 data were used to create landscape classification
    • Estimate coverage of LCP ecosystem across Fish Island

Interannual Variability


Results

It is unclear whether Fish Island is a net carbon sink.

  • Fairly consistent growing CO2 sink and CH4 source
    • Significant inter-annual variability for CO2
  • Shoulder season CO2 emissions may offset growing season uptake

Discussion

Temporal upscaling indicated significant interannual variability in growing season NEE and NME at Fish Island.

  • Climate warming could reduce the CO2 sink strength of Fish Island.
  • Compared to two similar low center polygon ecosystems with long-term eddy covariance records:
    • Fish Island is likely a weaker growing season CO2 sink and stronger growing season CH4 source than a low center polygon ecosystem near Utqiagvik, Alaska.
    • Fish Island may be both a stronger growing season CO2 sink CH4 source than a low center polygon ecosystem at Samoylov Island, in the Lena River Delta.

Overarching Conclusions

Both Illisarvik and Fish Island were growing season CO2 sinks and CH4 sources.

  • Illisarvik was a weak CH4 source with patches of very high fluxes in wet areas dominated by sedges
  • Fish Island is likely a stronger CH4 source than comparable ecosystems in Alaska and Siberia

Overarching Conclusions

Spatial heterogeneity was a significant factor influencing carbon fluxes at Fish Island and Illisarvik.

  • Both NEE and NME at Illisarvik varied significantly between different vegetation types
  • At Fish Island, polygonal microtopography had a significant influence over NME but had no influence over NEE.
  • Neural network analysis and footprint modelling can be combined with to help account for, and model the influence of, spatial heterogeneity on eddy covariance flux observations.

Future Research

  1. Long-term flux data needed:
    • Chapter 4 shows that interannual variability can have a significant impact on fluxes
    • Warming may reduce overall sink strength
  2. Cold season fluxes:
    • Extended senescent period could offset much of growing season uptake
  3. Methane fluxes:
    • Pressure pumping
    • Spatial variability and landscape scale changes
  4. Network architecture
    • Multi-layer models to simulate GPP and ER separately
    • Time lagged models

Reflections

Future work should focus on sustainable longer term flux monitoring

  • Short, resource intensive campaigns are a misallocation of resources
    • Tens of thousands of dollars worth of equipment, fuel, & labor for only 4 months of flux data
  • Prioritize engagement & involvement of local communities for more sustainable research
    • Technical training & employment will benefit communities
