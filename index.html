Framing the Problem

Arctic amplification is accelerating climate change in the northern high latitudes, leading to:

  • Permafrost degradation, extending growing seasons, etc.
    • Changing ecosystem carbon (C) balances
    • Influencing the rate of warming globally

Source: IPCC (2021)

Limited Observations

Eddy covariance (EC) is a method to measure carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4) fluxes.

  • Sparse coverage in the Arctic
    • Bias towards accessible sites
    • Canadian Arctic is under-represented

Sources: Walker et al. (2005); Pallandt et al. (2021)

Tundra Ecosystems

Harsh conditions stunt vegetation.

  • Summers are brief but productive
  • Winters are long, dark, and frigid

Tundra Ecosystems

Permafrost inhibits decomposition;
tundra soils are often C rich.

  • Active layer thaws seasonally
    • Allows root growth and microbial activity
  • Water tables are often elevated
    • Suppressed respiration
    • CH4 production

Tundra Carbon Balance

NEE of CO2 is the primary component of the C balance.

  • GPP: Gross Primary Productivity
    • Photosynthetic uptake of CO2
  • ER: Ecosystem Respiration
    • Autotrophic Respiration (RA)
    • Heterotrophic Respiration (RH)
  • NEE = ER - GPP
    • -NEE = uptake
    • +NEE = emission

Tundra Carbon Balance

NME of CH4 is an important secondary component in tundra wetlands.

  • Methanogenesis
    • CH4 production in anoxic soils
  • Methanotropy
    • CH4 consumption in aerobic soils
    • CH4 converted to CO2
  • NME = Methanogenesis - Methanotropy
    • -NME = uptake
    • +NME = emission

Machine Learning

A family of methods where algorithms build predictive models from experience.

  • Gap filling is essential for calculating C budgets from EC observations
    • Flux partitioning is the standard approach for NEE, no standard methods for NME
      • Polar summer > night-time partitioning not possible
      • Rapid phenology changes > day-time methods impractical too
  • Neural Network (NN) methods offer a flexible alternative
    • No prior assumptions
    • Universal approximators
      • Capable of mapping any continuous function to an arbitrary degree of accuracy

Discussion

Long-term observations are needed to better contextualize results.

  1. Compared to young DTLB (< 50 yrs) studied in Alaska:
    1. Greater shrub cover and higher CO2 uptake

  2. Illisarvik will continue to evolve, following one of two trajectories:
    1. Sedge dominated > NME will increase significantly
    2. Shrub dominated > NME will low
    3. Effects on NEE are less straightforward

Chapter 5: Conclusions

Summary of findings, key contributions, future research, and reflections

Summary of Findings

Both sites were growing season CO2 sinks and CH4 sources.

  • Illisarvik:
    • Greater CO2 uptake and less CH4 emission than other DTLB studied
  • Fish Island:
    • Higher CH4 fluxes than LCP ecosystems in Alaska and Siberia
    • Mixed results for CO2 uptake

Summary of Findings

Spatial heterogeneity had significant influence over fluxes at both sites.

  • Illisarvik:
    • Both NEE and NME varied between different vegetation types
  • Fish Island:
    • Polygonal microtopography had a significant influence over NME
    • Less significant influence over NEE

Methodological Contributions

Neural Networks are not "black-box" models when applied carefully.

  • The weights method helps us understand what the models are actually doing
    • To date, had not been applied to EC data
  • Mapping response functions is a key advantage over Random Forest Models
  • Meticulous application is the key to success
    • Repetition (ensembles, bootstrapping, etc.)
    • Cross validation and early stopping
  • Can be paired with footprint modelling to help account for spatial heterogeneity

Future Research

  • Long-term flux data needed:
    • Chapter 4 shows that interannual variability can have a significant impact on fluxes
    • Warming may reduce overall sink strength
  • Cold season fluxes:
    • Extended senescent period could offset much of growing season uptake
  • Methane fluxes:
    • Pressure pumping
    • Spatial variability and landscape scale changes
  • Network architecture
    • Multi-layer models to simulate GPP and ER separately
    • Time-lagged models to account for CH4 dynamics

Reflections

If our priority is just extracting information, the research only serves to perpetuate colonialism (Liboiron 2021).

  • Future work in the Arctic should focus on long-term flux monitoring
    • Short, resource intensive campaigns are not sustainable
  • Prioritize involvement of local stakeholders
    • Technical training & employment
    • Engagement and education
    • Community support = longer-term viability

Machine Learning Methods

Random Forests (RF):

  • Ensembles of bootstrapped regression trees
    • Easy to train and implement
    • Resistant to over fitting
  • Limitations
    • Incapable of extrapolation
    • No interpretable response functions

Neural Networks (NN):

  • Computationally intensive non-linear regression
    • Universal approximators
    • Prone to over fitting
  • Weights method (Gevery et al. 2003)
    • Inspection of response functions
    • Verify physical plausibility

Chapter 2

Neural Network models were used to:

  • Identify the dominant flux drivers by iteratively building more complex models
    • Climate, soil, and source area were used as model inputs
    • "1 Standard Error" rule identified the most parsimonious models
  • Selected sets of inputs for CO2 and CH4 were then used to gap fill the respective flux time series to estimate NEE and NME

Chapter 2

  • NEE: good agreement with FCO2 observations (r2 = 0.91)
    • PPFD was the primary driver of GPP
      • Modulated by VPD
    • VWC influenced ER
    • FShrub had weak influence
  • NME: reasonable agreement with FCH4 observations (r2 = 0.62)
    • FSedge was had strongest influence
      • Along with FShrub (strong correlation)
    • VWC and Ts drove fluxes
    • U had a weak influence
